IAMs: Toward Common Practices

 

This benchmarking exercise analyzes the accountability mechanisms of seven international financial institutions (IFIs):

  1. African Development Bank (AfDB) Independent Review Mechanism (IRM) [AFDB-IRM]
  2. Asian Development Bank (ADB) Accountability Mechanism (AM) [ADB-AM]
  3. European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) Project Complaint Mechanism (PCM) [EBRD-PCM]
  4. European Investment Bank (EIB) Complaints Mechanism (CM) [EIB-CM]
  5. Inter-American Development Bank (IADB) Independent Consultation and Investigation Mechanism (MICI) [IADB-MICI]
  6. International Finance Corporation (IFC) / Multilateral Investment Guarantee Agency (MIGA) Compliance Advisor Ombudsman (CAO) [IFC/MIGA-CAO]
  7. United Nations Development Program (UNDP) Social and Environmental Compliance Review and Stakeholder Response Mechanism (SECR-SRM) [UNDP-SERC/SRM]
  8. World Bank (WB) Inspection Panel (IP) [WB-IP]

The analysis is conducted against the underlying principles of accountability mechanisms: fairness, accessibility, independence, transparency, professionalism, and effectiveness.

Toward a Normative Definition of ‘Good Practice’

In reviewing the practices of IAMs, one can identify ‘established’ and ‘common practices’:

It is an established practice… When *nearly* all IAMs follow the same practice.
It is a common practice… When *the majority* of IAMs follow the same practice.
There are no common practices… When there is no clear majority of practice among IAMs.

The extent to which ‘established’ and ‘common’ practices can be considered ‘good practice’, however, is less apparent.

For example:

  • It is a common practice that Board / Presidential approval is required for initiating a compliance investigation / appraisal, but is this a ‘good practice’?
  • There is no common practice regarding involving complainants in the formulation of institutional action plans for remedying compliance violations. Would it be?
  • It is a common practice that IAM monitoring mandates derive from the decision/approval of an institutional authority, but is this ‘good practice’?


A Review of IAM Complaint Handling Practices


Registration

It is an established practice that IAMs have autonomy to register a complaint without Board / Presidential approval.

  • All IAMs reviewed (7/7) follow this practice.

Eligibility

Dispute Resolution / Problem-Solving

It is an established practice that IAMs determine the eligibility of a complaint for dispute resolution independently of Board / Presidential approval.

  • Most IAMs reviewed (5 of 6) follow this practice.

Compliance

It is a common practice that Board / Presidential approval is required for initiating a compliance investigation / appraisal.

  • Most IAMs reviewed (5 of 7) follow this practice.

Redress

Compliance

It is an established practice that institutional management prepares an action plan in response to IAM finding of non-compliance

  • All IAMs reviewed (7/7) follow this practice.

It is a common practice that IAMs are involved in the formulation of institutional action plans for remedying compliance violations.

  • Most IAMs reviewed (5/7) follow this practice.

There is no common practice regarding involving complainants in the formulation of institutional action plans for remedying compliance violations.

  • Some IAMs reviewed (3/7) follow this practice.

Reporting of Outcomes

Compliance

It is a common practice that required for release of a compliance investigation report does not involve Board / Presidential clearance.

  • Most IAMs reviewed (5/7) follow this practice.

Monitoring

Compliance

It is a common practice that IAM monitoring mandates derive from the decision/approval of an institutional authority.

  • Most IAMs reviewed (5/7) follow this practice.
